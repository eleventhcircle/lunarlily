Sometime around mid-April I was itching for something to play after work. I didn't have a ton of free time and wasn't in the mood for anything live-service so I ended up scrolling through my backlog of game demos on Steam, hoping something would catch my eye and save me from aimlessly scrolling for an hour before bed. And indeed something did.

Diorama Break is a tactics RPG with a hefty amount of disrespect for the fourth wall separating you from the world behind the screen; the "break" in the title can be taken quite literally. This is a game where you (yeah! 🫵 YOU!) are just as much a character in the story as the guy you're driving around.

His name is Pro and he's a piece of work. I want to study him in a lab.

This is the guy you drive around, by the way. He's a rather unenthusiastic Chosen One and for reasons unknown you have become his Patron, giving you full access to his thoughts and an invasive amount of control over his actions. Together you're going to save the world! Somehow. Probably? It'll work out one way or another, I'm sure...

I like stories that break the fourth wall and play with meta, and I think Diorama Break does so with a lot of character. The writing doesn't take itself too seriously and isn't afraid to poke fun at itself (or at you). Pro, for his part, is a sarcastic little shit and fairly disinterested in the role he was raised to fulfill. Luckily you have plenty of opportunity to jab right back at him, if you want to (and he doesn't take teasing well lol). All of your interaction with the Diorama and its inhabitants must happen through Pro - you are unable to interact with the world directly - and he proves occasionally willing to misrepresent you in order to get what he wants. Which, in the demo, is to get the hell out of town as fast as possible. (He's a pretty earnest guy overall though, so the dynamic between him and you never becomes outright hostile.)

The relationship between you and Pro is very central to the story; how you talk to and treat him will impact how many events play out. For example, in my first playthrough I was (mostly) nice to him, so he grew to trust me faster than he might have otherwise. This translated into a boon during the final boss fight: Pro listened without question to my warning about an incoming attack from an enemy he couldn't see but I could, fully dodging what would have otherwise killed him. In a different playthrough I was a much more of a jackass toward him so he trusted me less; in that same battle, he didn't react fast enough to my warning because he didn't really believe that the guy was there at all. He took a partial hit because of it, and we had to face the boss at like half-health from the start which sucked real bad lol.

This is the boss you try to warn him about.
One could say that the way you treat Pro certainly has... Consequences ;]

The imbalance of knowledge between you and Pro intrigues me. He (and everyone inside the Diorama) are very much aware of our world, but that level of awareness does not extend both ways - we aren't told anything by the two curators(?) Mr. and Ms. Window outside the Diorama about what to expect once we connect, or what "connecting" to it even really means. You can learn some world history if you stop by the town library after connecting with Pro, but nothing you learn gives any meaningful insight into your role as the Patron. After some poking and prodding you realize Pro can't really give you any insight about your role or his world either: he grew up sequestered away in his village and has next to no practical knowledge about the lands beyond his home. Everything he knows comes from school lessons and what the adults in his life have told him (and just how much do we trust them, anyway?). Now, Pro can't easily get away with lying or obfuscating things from us, since we are quite literally in his head, but I'm really curious to see if anyone tries to take advantage of this gap. Ultimately, we only know as much about the world as he does - so can we ever be sure that neither of us is being manipulated or played for fools? I hope this becomes a Thing we have to deal with later in the story.

It's worth detouring for the library btw. They got yuri in there...

I am absolutely Piss Awful at the combat but I still think it's fun (even if I wish for a bit of difficulty tuning). It's turn-based, with each turn having six "beats" within which you and your enemies can act. It's not really a rhythm game, per se, but there is a rhythm to combat. Hitting an enemy will Break them, interrupting their action for the rest of the turn; the same goes for Pro, too, so try not to get hit lol. I'll be honest, most of my time on my first playthrough was spent getting my shit rocked > respawning at the last save point > running back to where I died so I could get my shit rocked again, until I figured out how to manage all the different kinds of enemies. The demo doesn't do the best job of tutorializing you so a lot of my understanding came from just fucking around and finding out. Big fan of enemies being able to Break and damage each other - I was delighted the first time this happened in combat and it helped me navigate busier fights; I don't know if I could have made it out of a couple areas without using the enemies against each other. I genuinely lost count of how many times I died and I couldn't tell you how much of that was my own skill issue versus the combat just being too difficult? I'm leaning more towards skill issue right now since Mr. and Ms. Window outright admit that they don't have any new dialogue for you past the first death, because they didn't expect anyone to die more than once lol. More time in combat meant more time listening to the OST so honestly I wasn't too upset.

Diorama Break scratched my brain in such a specific and fulfilling way that I almost found it startling. Not that I expected to dislike it or anything; rather, it just clicked with me way more than I anticipated. There's nothing quite like finding a piece of art that delights you so thoroughly! The dialogue branching and responsive events lend a great deal of replayability; if I was able to wring multiple clears out of the demo alone, imagine how much I could get out of the full game, yknow?

As of posting this, the Kickstarter for Diorama Break has been fully funded and there's still 5 days left! They didn't have a runaway campaign but funding came in steadily, and I'm curious to see if the campaign hits the first stretch goal - it's close right now, about $1k away. I wish the wait until the full release wasn't so long (current estimate is 2029) but I don't want the dev team to rush, I'm just impatient because I'm excited. I backed at a high enough tier to get beta access so at least I'll have some crumbs for the next few years but ough... orz
