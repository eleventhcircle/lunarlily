Been a while since I last yapped about books and I have quite a few checked out right now, so let's change that!

Ye Olde Booke Stacke (feat. one movie I checked out on a whim). I got a cute bookend at Daiso to help keep my borrowed books upright on the table, but there's too much clutter on there to put them up properly right now... hence, The Stacke.

Recently I've been on a light novel kick. My library allows patrons five purchase requests per month, and I've been using mine to slowly (but surely!) expand their collection. I'm quite picky about what I'll read (not really an isekai fan, for example, and I'm not fond of first person POV), but with enough trial and error I found some series that I'm excited to try! It tickles me that my library categorizes light novels as teen books...

  • 7th Time Loop: The Villainess Enjoys a Carefree Life Married to Her Worst Enemy! by Touko Amekawa - I read the first volume of the manga on Libby while waiting to pick up my partner from the airport. I knew nothing about it going in but enjoyed it a lot more than I expected! My library already had the first LN volume so I put a hold in for it a couple days later.
  • Kusunoki's Garden of Gods, by Enju - This one gives me Natsume Yuujinchou vibes, maaaaybe crossed with a little Kamisama Hajimemashita? I could be wrong on the second part but either way I'm looking forward to trying it.
  • Shanti, by Shinano Sano - Light novel adaptation of a Vocaloid song by the same name, produced by wotaku. 1920s mafia story set in Chinatown - I'm sure if you listen + read the lyrics that you can get a sense of the vibes lol.
  • The Case Files of Jeweler Richard, by Nanako Tsujimura - Modern-day queer mystery series. I don't know a ton about it but the premise intrigued me enough to put in a purchase request. I'm pretty sure it was turned into an anime as well? (Post-google edit: indeed it was!)
  • Sentenced to be a Hero: The Prison Records of Penal Hero Unit 9004, by Rocket Shokai - This is the one I'm most uncertain about. It's interesting in theory - a world where heroism is a punishment? Tell me more - but I don't know if I'll jive with the writing style... it's written in first person which doesn't help things for me lol. It ranked in the top 10 of Kono light novel ga sugoi! in 2023 which surely has to count for something?
  • The Other World's Books Depend on the Bean Counter: Holy Maiden Summoning Improvement Plan, by Yatsuki Wakatsu - Another where I read volume one of the manga first to test the waters before committing to a purchase request. This series has been on my radar for a while - I've seen copies of the manga at the bookstore a bunch of times but I never really looked into what it's about. Jokes on me for that; I ended up liking it enough to give the LN a try. Men who can lock in for one specific thing but are otherwise kinda soppy and pathetic compel me, what can I say. (Also what is it with LNs and long titles man...)

The nonfiction I have checked out right now is quite a bit less by comparison... I read nonfiction much slower, though, so I try not to keep too many on my shelf at once; I get overwhelmed otherwise.

  • Friends in Common: Radical Friendship and Everyday Solidarities, by Laura C. Forster & Joel White - My partner showed this to me a few months ago and I put in a purchase request for it! Finally came in last month. I thought something with a more hopeful/positive topic would do me some good right now :]
  • Amusing Ourselves to Death: Public Discourse in the Age of Show Business, by Neil Postman - I came across a recommendation for this while browsing around a forum (I think I was on 32-bit Cafe? Can't for the life of me find the post in question, though it may have since been deleted). The timing could not be more appropriate, I think, considering the whole... (vague gesturing) The Everything nowadays. I have a feeling it'll make me pace around my room a lot.

Last but possibly the most exciting (to me): craft books! I love checking out books about all sorts of different topics; it's fun to expand my horizons, and if I never get farther than skimming through a few chapters and deciding it's not for me, that's OK! No money lost on my part and I helped my library's circulation numbers :] I have the same mentality with checking out cookbooks too lol

  • The Knitting Book, by Frederica Patmore & Vikki Haffenden - I came across this at the craft store (though it was a previous edition) and thought it looked pretty neat while flipping through. I taught myself how to crochet a couple years ago (bad at it but love it) and figured I could try multiclassing into knitting too. I didn't want to commit 40 entire dollars to a book without knowing if I'll even like knitting; lucky for me, the library had the newer edition available to borrow! I picked up The Sewing Book and The Quilting Book from the same series (pubished by DK, an imprint of Penguin Random House) for fun. Please forgive the PRH link; Storygraph doesn't have the 2026 edition listed yet :[
  • Make, Sew, and Mend, by Bernadette Banner - I'm a far more confident machine sewist than handsewist, and I trust Miss Banner's skill after watching many of her videos over the years. If I'm going to learn how to fell a seam from anyone, I want it to be from her.
  • Ahead of the Curve, by Jenny Rushmore - I find myself grumpily searching for plus size pattern books and patternmakers after I have to buy clothes for whatever reason (in this case, a funeral) because I loathe the process of finding things that fit well, look nice, and don't cost half a paycheck. It actually does have the advertised collection of patterns in the back which is awesome, I wasn't sure if the library would keep those with the book itself or remove them for circulation. If I like any patterns out of here, I can just trace the necessary pieces onto my roll of butcher's paper!
  • Handmade Books at Home, by Chanel Ly - Ly is a bookbinder I've watched for many years on Youtube! Initially I learned the basics from Sea Lemon (also on YT) and handbound a few books as gifts for friends and family when I was in college. I really enjoyed the process! My uncle even helped me turn a couple old wooden chopping boards into a book press (which has come in clutch for more than just making books lol). I've had an itch to pick it back up lately, so where better to start than with a trusted teacher?
  • Story and Dialogue, by Robert McKee - Picked up on recommendation from a comic artist I deeply admire. I hope to walk from these with a plethora of practical advice on the areas of storycrafting that I'm weak in, and also enough confidence to start a project simmering in the back of my mind for years...
  • Manga in Theory and Practice, by Hirohiko Araki - Stumbled across this in the wilds of the library's recommendations page (a dangerous place, don't visit lightly). Like the McKee books, I thought this could help me too with that project I just mentioned - and if it doesn't, it's still cool to pick the brain of such a respected creator and learn about their process!

On a related note, my library's summer reading challenge started on the first of June. It's put some of the wind back in my literary sails! Maybe a little too much wind... I've already finished four books this month, putting me well over halfway to the 1000 minute goal. How many of those four were from this list? Not a single one! Instead I made a small dent in my personal collection.

See, I started the Secrets of the Silent Witch series last year and absolutely loved the first two volumes, so I've been hunting down the rest of the series. I picked up 6 and 7 when I spotted them at the store - I even found a copy of the interlude volume 4.5 - but for months it was basically impossible to buy volumes 3-5 anywhere online or off. I suspect the last printing that Yen Press ordered sold out completely after the anime debuted last summer and they were slow to order a fresh run, leaving me stuck in limbo on a deeply annoying cliffhanger until they started distributing again. It put me in a pretty bad reading slump, honestly... until a few weeks ago, when my beloved bookshop.org sent me a restock notification! After waiting so long to get my hands on them, I couldn't hold myself back; I read volumes 3, 4, 4.5, and 5 all in one day.

("But Luna," I hear you ask, "if they were sold out of physical copies for such a long time, why didn't you just read the missing volumes digitally?" To which I say: yeah you're absolutely right, I could've just got the ebooks and kept reading right away. I thought about it quite a few times, too... but unfortunately I'm stubborn (and kind of cheap lol) - Silent Witch is one of the LN series I chose to collect physically, and I didn't want to buy the same books twice when I could just get the print copies I really wanted. So instead I just grumpily waited until they came back in stock...)

Lucina and Robin guard my cherished light novels. I left a space for volume 8 of Silent Witch - it's set to release in September!

Anywho, back to the reading challenge: Anyone who logs at least 1000 minutes of reading before the end of the challenge wins a free book of their choice from a fairly big catalog that rotates annually. I'm not sure what to pick as my prize this year... They've got some interesting options on offer, but it'll partly depend on what's still available by the time I cross the finish line. At this rate I don't think it'll take much longer, ehehe...
